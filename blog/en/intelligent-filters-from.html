The search problem no one talks about

You know the information is in there. You added it. Three weeks ago, or maybe two days ago — somewhere in your notes, your tasks, your projects. So you scroll. You search by keyword. You get seventeen results, most of them irrelevant. You spend more time looking than the task was worth.

Most tools treat search as a last resort. From treats it as a first-class feature — one that understands what kind of thing you're looking for, not just what word you typed.

Filters that think in categories, not keywords

From knows the difference between a task and a note. Between something due today and something with no date. Between items inside a specific project context and items floating at the top level. That structural awareness is what makes its filter system genuinely fast.

Instead of a search bar that returns a wall of matches, From gives you chips — small, composable filters you stack together. Each chip narrows the view. You're not searching; you're describing what you want to see.

Stack chips to narrow results instantly — type, date, context, status.

The available dimensions cover the full shape of your work:

  • Type — show only tasks, only notes, only events, only resources
  • Date — today, this week, overdue, has a date, no date
  • Status — pending, done, in progress
  • Context — items tagged to a specific area of your life or work

Each of these works alone. They also work together. A filter for tasks + overdue + work context gives you exactly what's blocking you right now — nothing else.

Concrete examples: what this actually looks like

Here's where the difference becomes clear. Say it's Monday morning and you want to triage everything that slipped last week. You tap Type: Task, then Date: Overdue. Done. That's your triage list, pulled instantly from everything inside From — regardless of which project it lives under.

Or you're preparing for a meeting and need to pull up all the notes and resources you've tagged to a specific client context. Tap Context: [client name], then Type: Note. Two chips. A clean, relevant view in under three seconds.

Filters apply across your entire tree — across projects, dates, and contexts.

Or maybe you're reviewing what you actually got done this week. Status: Done + Date: This week. That's your weekly review, no manual log required.

From doesn't just search text — it filters by structure. That's the difference between a tool that finds words and a tool that finds things.

Save any filter as a panel

The part that turns a useful feature into a workflow change: any filter combination you build can be saved as a panel.

A panel is a persistent view that lives in your sidebar. You name it, you pin it, and it's always there — already filtered, already focused, no setup required each time you open it.

This is how you build your actual workspace in From. Not by organizing folders or moving things around — by defining the views that matter to your specific work and making them permanent.

  • A Today panel: tasks + pending + due today
  • A Deep work panel: tasks + a specific context + no date (unscheduled backlog)
  • A Weekly review panel: tasks + done + this week
  • A Resource library panel: type resource + a project context

Each one is a lens. You're not managing different files or views manually. From holds everything in one tree, and panels let you see it exactly the way you need to at any given moment.

Saved panels in the sidebar — each one a filter combination you use regularly.

How to use it: the practical workflow

  1. Start with the chip bar. Every view in From has access to the filter chips at the top. Click any chip to activate it. Click another to combine. The view updates instantly.
  2. Stack chips until the result is exactly what you want. There's no search button, no loading. The filter is live.
  3. When you find a combination you use often, save it. Give it a name that matches the workflow it serves. It becomes a panel in your sidebar.
  4. Open panels directly. Your saved views are one click away. You don't rebuild the filter each time.

The entire system is designed to be low-friction. You're not configuring a database view or writing a filter query. You're picking from a small set of dimensions that map directly to how you already think about your work.

No search anxiety

The practical outcome of all this is that you stop second-guessing where you put things. From's filters work across your entire tree — across every project, every context, every date range — in a single, instant operation. You stop scrolling and start trusting the system. That's the difference between a tool you use and a tool you rely on.
