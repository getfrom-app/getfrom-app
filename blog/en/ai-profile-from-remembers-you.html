Your AI profile: From remembers you without being told

From builds a profile from what you write — only what lasts. No noise, no clutter.

From Blog · 5 junio 2026 · 5 min read

Every tool you use asks you to set it up. Fill in your name, your goals, your preferences. Tell it what matters. Then tell it again next week, because it forgot. From doesn't work that way.

From builds a picture of who you are from what you actually write — and it keeps only what sticks. Not every fleeting thought, not every temporary note. The things that define how you work, what you're focused on, what you care about long-term. It learns by watching, not by asking.

The problem with "smart" tools that don't remember

Most productivity software is stateless. Every session starts from zero. You can have a hundred tasks in the system and the assistant still asks you what you're working on. It has no sense of who you are, what matters to you, or what context surrounds each item you write.

The result is constant friction. You spend more time briefing your tools than using them. You explain the same priorities over and over. You give context that should already be obvious. It's exhausting — and it defeats the purpose of having a system at all.

From solves this at the root. Instead of asking you to define your context, it extracts it from what you write, over time, automatically.

How From builds your profile

When you write in From — tasks, notes, ideas, plans — certain things recur. Projects you return to. People you mention. Goals that show up in multiple places. From tracks these patterns quietly and builds a profile from them.

This isn't keyword matching or a simple tag system. From identifies what's durable versus what's transient. A task that appears once and gets done doesn't define you. A project you've been building toward for months does. The profile captures the latter, not the former.

From's Magic surface — where your profile shapes every response

The profile includes things like:

  • Active projects — what you're genuinely working on right now, inferred from task density and recency
  • Key people — names that appear repeatedly across your notes and tasks
  • Goals and priorities — themes that show up consistently in how you frame your work
  • Working patterns — when you tend to plan, review, or capture ideas

None of this requires you to declare anything. You write, From observes, the profile updates.

What changes when From knows you

The difference becomes visible the moment you open Magic — From's integrated assistant. Instead of a blank slate, you get responses that already know your context. Ask "what should I focus on today?" and From doesn't generate a generic answer. It looks at what you've written, what's overdue, what you've been building toward, and responds from that foundation.

Magic responds with full context — no briefing required

Concrete examples of what this unlocks:

  1. Contextual suggestions. When you're about to plan your week, From already knows which projects are active and which are stalled. It doesn't suggest generic tasks — it surfaces the specific things that matter to your current situation.
  2. Smarter search. When you search or filter your notes, results are weighted by relevance to your profile. Things connected to your active priorities float up. Old, disconnected items stay out of the way.
  3. Continuity between sessions. You can close From, come back three days later, and pick up exactly where you were. Not because you bookmarked anything, but because your profile persists and shapes what you see first.

The right kind of memory

There's a real risk with tools that try to remember everything: they become noise machines. Every throwaway note, every abandoned idea, every temporary plan accumulates into a profile that reflects the past, not the present.

From handles this through recency and signal strength. Recent activity carries more weight. Things you act on repeatedly matter more than things you write once. Items you mark done or delete fade from the profile naturally. The result is a profile that stays current without any manual curation from you.

You don't manage the profile. You just use From — and the profile takes care of itself.

How to get the most out of it

Because From builds your profile from natural writing, the best way to strengthen it is simply to use From consistently for your actual work. A few habits that accelerate the process:

  • Write in context. When you capture a task, give it a sentence of context. Not for From — for yourself. But From reads it too, and it helps the profile stay accurate.
  • Use the daily note. The today view is where From anchors your current focus. Writing there daily gives the profile a strong signal about what actually matters right now.
  • Don't fight the hierarchy. Organizing notes under project nodes helps From understand relationships. A task nested under a project is more informative than a flat list of items.

A tool that keeps up with you

From's profile isn't a setup step — it's an ongoing layer that runs underneath everything you do. The longer you use it, the more accurate it becomes, and the less friction you experience. You stop explaining yourself to your tools. You just work, and From keeps up.

That's the version of smart software that actually earns the label. Not a feature you toggle on — a capability that grows with you, invisibly, every day you use it.
