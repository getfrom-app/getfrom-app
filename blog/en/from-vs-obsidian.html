Fromly vs Obsidian: which do you need in 2026?

Both respect your data. But one is for writers who want to stay in the flow — and one isn't.

Fromly Blog · 5 junio 2026 · 5 min read

Two tools that respect your data — but solve different problems

Obsidian and Fromly are both local-first, privacy-respecting tools built around plain text and hierarchy. That's where the similarities end.

Obsidian is a knowledge base. It's built for researchers, writers, and developers who want to map connections between ideas across hundreds of notes over years. It rewards configuration. It rewards patience. If you enjoy spending a Sunday afternoon tuning plugins, Obsidian is genuinely excellent at what it does.

Fromly is an outliner built around your daily flow. It doesn't ask you to configure anything. You open it, start typing, and it already understands whether you're writing a task, a note, a quick idea, or something you want to track — without requiring you to tag it, drag it, or classify it manually.

The real question isn't which app is better. It's which one disappears when you're working — and which one becomes the work itself.

Where Obsidian earns its reputation

If you're building a long-term knowledge graph, Obsidian is hard to beat. Its backlink system, graph view, and plugin ecosystem are mature and powerful. It's the right tool when:

  • You're researching a complex topic over months and need to connect ideas across dozens of notes
  • You want full control over your vault structure, file formats, and sync method
  • You're a developer or technical writer who's comfortable with Markdown and doesn't mind occasional setup friction
  • Your primary output is long-form written content, not day-to-day tasks

Obsidian rewards the person who loves the system. There's nothing wrong with that — but it's worth being honest about who that person is.

Where Fromly changes the equation

Most people don't need a knowledge graph. They need to capture what's in their head, plan their day, and find things again without friction. That's the problem Fromly solves — and it solves it by understanding what you write, not just storing it.

Fromly's outliner keeps your notes, tasks, and ideas in a single connected tree — no separate apps needed.

When you write "Call the accountant tomorrow at 10" in Fromly, it detects the task and the date. When you type a quick thought under a project node, it stays there — in context — not lost in a flat list of files. The structure you'd spend hours building in Obsidian with plugins and templates emerges naturally in Fromly as you write.

Fromly's hierarchy is also different. Instead of markdown files linked by backlinks, Fromly uses a live node tree: every item — task, note, event, resource — lives in the same structure, visible and navigable in one place. There's no friction between capture and organisation.

The daily workflow difference

Here's a concrete scenario: it's Monday morning. You have three client calls, a project deadline, and three things you meant to do last Friday.

In Obsidian, you'd open your daily note template, manually transfer tasks from previous days, check your project files, and build your focus list. That's fine if the ritual energises you. But it takes 10–20 minutes before you're actually working.

In Fromly, you open the app. Your pending tasks are already there. The planner shows what's due. Magic — Fromly's built-in assistant — can surface what you haven't finished or suggest what to prioritise, based on your actual notes and tasks, not generic prompts.

Fromly's planner gives you a clear view of your day — tasks, events, and priorities in one place.

The difference is that Fromly doesn't require a workflow. It is the workflow.

What Fromly understands that Obsidian doesn't

Obsidian stores text. Fromly reads it. When you're inside a project node and write a task, From knows it belongs to that project. When you add a date, it appears in your calendar view. When a task has been sitting undone for three days, filters surface it without you having to search.

Magic answers questions based on your actual notes — not the internet, not a generic model.

This isn't "AI" in the vague sense. It's context-aware detection built into the editor itself. Fromly doesn't need you to describe what something is — it figures it out from how and where you write it.

A few things Fromly does that Obsidian requires plugins for:

  1. Task detection from plain prose — no need to write checkboxes manually
  2. Calendar view built in — see events and tasks on a timeline without setup
  3. Smart filters — surface pending, overdue, or tagged items instantly
  4. Magic assistant — ask questions about your own notes, get answers from your data
  5. Planner — drag tasks into your day, see your week, plan without switching apps

Which one do you actually need?

Choose Obsidian if your primary need is long-term knowledge management, deep research, or building a personal wiki that spans years of thinking.

Choose Fromly if you want to stay in the flow — capture fast, plan your day, track what matters, and let the tool handle the structure. Fromly is for people who want to think, not organise.

Both tools respect your data and work offline. But only one of them gets out of your way the moment you open it. If you've ever looked at your Obsidian vault and thought "this is impressive but I'm not actually using it" — Fromly is probably what you needed from the start.
