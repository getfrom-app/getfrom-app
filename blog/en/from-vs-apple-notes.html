From vs Apple Notes: when the native app is no longer enough

Apple Notes is great until your notes become hundreds. Then you need a brain, not a folder.

From Blog · 5 junio 2026 · 5 min read

Apple Notes gets you started. Then you outgrow it.

Apple Notes is one of the best default apps on any platform. It's fast, syncs perfectly, and it's always there. For grocery lists, quick thoughts, meeting notes — it's fine. More than fine.

But at some point, you have 300 notes. Then 600. You're searching for something you wrote three weeks ago and getting twelve results back. You remember writing it but not what you called it. You start a new note because you can't find the old one. Duplicates appear. Folders become a filing project you never finish.

Apple Notes was designed to capture. It was never designed to think.

The folder problem

The fundamental issue with Apple Notes — and most note-taking apps — is that they ask you to organize before you understand what you're organizing. You create a note, then you have to decide: which folder does this go in? Work? Personal? Projects? Ideas?

Most of the time, the honest answer is: I don't know yet. So you dump it in a generic folder or in "All Notes" and forget it.

Over time, your notes become a graveyard. Things you wrote but never found again. Tasks mixed with thoughts mixed with reference material. No structure because the structure you planned never held.

The problem isn't that you're disorganized. The problem is that Apple Notes forces you to organize manually, every time, with no help.

What changes with From

From works differently from the ground up. Instead of flat notes in folders, everything lives in a connected tree. Notes, tasks, events, resources — they're all nodes you can nest, link, and move. Your thinking has structure without you having to impose it manually.

In From, everything has a place in a connected hierarchy — no orphaned notes.

But the bigger difference is what From does with what you write.

When you type something in From, it doesn't just store the text. It reads it. Write "dentist appointment next Tuesday at 10" and From turns it into an event. Write "buy milk, eggs, and bread" and From turns those into tasks. Write a meeting note with action items and From identifies the tasks inside it automatically.

You don't tag. You don't categorize. You don't format. You write, and From figures out the rest.

Concrete examples Apple Notes can't match

Tasks buried in notes

In Apple Notes, if you write a meeting summary with follow-up actions, those actions live inside a note. They're invisible to any task view. You have to manually re-enter them somewhere else — another app, a reminder, a checklist. That's friction, and friction is why things get forgotten.

In From, those follow-up items are detected as tasks the moment you write them. They show up in your task filters, in your planner, in your daily view. You wrote them once. From handles the rest.

Tasks detected from natural writing appear automatically in filtered views — no re-entry needed.

Finding things without remembering what you called them

Apple Notes search finds text. That's useful, but it relies on you remembering specific words. From has filters that work by meaning and type. You can ask From to show you all notes from this week, or all pending tasks under a specific project, or everything tagged as a resource. You're not searching — you're filtering a structured system.

Planning your day

Apple Notes has no planner. You can write a to-do list, but it doesn't connect to your calendar, it doesn't surface what's overdue, and it doesn't help you decide what to do next. From has a built-in planner that pulls your tasks and events together. Your day is visible in one place without switching between Notes and Calendar and Reminders.

How to make the switch practical

You don't need to migrate everything at once. The most effective way to start with From is to begin with today.

  1. Open From's daily note every morning. Write what you need to do, what you're thinking about, what you need to remember. Don't format it — just write.
  2. Let From sort it. Tasks, events, and notes will be detected and categorized automatically. Your daily view becomes a real capture layer.
  3. Use filters instead of folders. When you need to find something, don't browse — filter by type, date, or status. The structure is already there.

For Apple Notes users, the shift takes a few days. The main adjustment is trusting that you don't need to organize as you write. From handles that part.

The daily note is your starting point — write freely and From structures it for you.

Apple Notes isn't going anywhere — but it has a ceiling

Apple Notes will always be useful for quick captures when you're in Apple's ecosystem and need zero friction. It's not going away, and it shouldn't.

But if your notes have become a pile you're afraid to search, if your tasks are scattered across five different places, or if you spend more time maintaining your system than using it — you've hit the ceiling.

From is what comes after that ceiling. It's not a fancier folder system. It's a tool that reads what you write and turns it into something you can actually use. Start with one day's worth of notes and see what it does with them.
