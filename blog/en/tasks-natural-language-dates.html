The date picker problem nobody talks about

You're in the middle of a thought. You type "call Ana on Thursday" and then everything stops. You open a date picker, click the right month, find Thursday, confirm. Three seconds gone. Focus broken. You're no longer capturing — you're managing a UI.

Multiply that by every task you create in a day. It adds up fast. And the worst part? The information was already there, in plain English, when you typed "Thursday."

Fromly skips the picker entirely. It reads what you write.

Fromly understands natural language dates as you type — no menus, no clicks, no extra steps. Write "meeting with Ana on Monday" and the date is set. Done.

How Fromly reads your words

When you type a note or task in Fromly, it doesn't just store text. It reads it. Expressions like "next Tuesday," "tomorrow morning," "in two weeks," or "end of the month" are parsed automatically and converted into a real scheduled date on your task.

You don't trigger anything. You don't press a shortcut or wait for a suggestion. Fromly detects the date reference as part of understanding what you wrote, and sets it silently in the background.

Fromly sets the date from the text — no picker needed.

This works across a wide range of formats:

  • "Send the report by Friday" → scheduled for this Friday
  • "Call the accountant next Monday" → scheduled for next Monday
  • "Review the contract in 3 days" → scheduled 3 days from now
  • "Team meeting on June 12" → scheduled for June 12
  • "Dentist appointment tomorrow at 10" → scheduled for tomorrow

No special syntax. No tags. No brackets. Just write like you normally would, and Fromly handles the rest.

Why this matters more than it seems

Most productivity tools separate two things that naturally go together: what you need to do, and when. You write the task, then you schedule it. That's two steps, two modes, two moments of friction.

Fromly treats the date as part of the task description — because in natural language, it always is. "Meeting with Ana on Monday" is one thought. Fromly captures it as one action.

This is especially useful when you're capturing fast: during a call, after a quick conversation, while reviewing your inbox. You don't want to slow down to fiddle with a calendar widget. You want to get the thought out and move on.

Tasks appear in the planner automatically once a date is set from your text.

What happens after the date is set

Once Fromly reads the date from your text, the task flows naturally into the rest of the system. It appears in the planner on the right day. It shows up in your agenda view. It's filterable by date. Nothing extra needed from you.

If you wrote "meeting with Ana on Monday," that task will be waiting for you on Monday — not floating in some unscheduled backlog. Fromly took care of placing it where it belongs.

The planner in Fromly gives you a day-by-day view of everything scheduled. Tasks that came in through natural language sit alongside tasks scheduled manually — they're identical to the system. The only difference is how little effort it took to create them.

What about ambiguous dates?

Fromly makes reasonable assumptions based on context. "Friday" means this coming Friday, not the one three weeks out. "Next Monday" means the Monday of next week, not this one. These are the same assumptions a human assistant would make.

If you need to adjust, you can. Tap the task, change the date. But in practice, Fromly gets it right the vast majority of the time — because it's applying the same logic you'd apply if someone asked you what "next Monday" means.

Using it in practice

The best way to get value from this feature is to stop thinking about it as a feature. Just write your tasks naturally, the way you'd describe them to someone. Don't change your language to fit the tool — let the tool fit your language.

  1. Capture the task as a full sentence: "Send invoice to client by Thursday"
  2. Fromly reads it and schedules it for Thursday
  3. It appears in your planner on Thursday
  4. You move on

That's it. No extra steps. No switching contexts. The capture is the scheduling.

The fastest way to schedule a task is to write it the way you'd say it out loud. Fromly does the translation from language to date automatically.

Conclusion

Date pickers exist because most tools can't read. Fromly can. When you write "call Ana on Monday," that's already complete information — and Fromly treats it that way. The result is a capture flow that's genuinely fast, low-friction, and built around how you actually think.
