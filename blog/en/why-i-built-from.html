Why I Built From

Ten years trying Notion, Obsidian, Roam, Bear... and none lasted more than six months. This is the story of what went wrong, what I learned, and how that led me to build something of my own.

Alberto Lezaun · June 5, 2026 · 10 min read

I'm Alberto. I built From.

This is the story of why. Not the marketing version — the real one.

The app graveyard

Notion, version 1. I built a perfect project database. Took me three hours to set up. Lasted two weeks using it.

Obsidian. The plugins were incredible — also a bottomless pit. I spent more time installing plugins than writing notes. After a month of setup, the system was «ready». And then I barely used it.

Roam Research. Fifteen dollars a month. The block syntax took me a week to understand. When I finally understood it, I realized it wasn't for me.

Bear. Beautiful. Clean Markdown. No real sync between devices without paying. Goodbye. Apple Notes. Simple and free — and completely useless once you have more than twenty notes to find.

The story always repeats. I start with enthusiasm, build the «perfect» system, use it for two weeks, then stop. The empty system stares at me from the screen as a reminder that I failed again.

For years I thought the problem was me. That I lacked enough discipline, that I hadn't found the right system, that if I tried harder I'd manage to keep it up.

It wasn't me. It was something else.

The real problem: thinking before capturing

Notion has databases, views, templates, formulas, table relations. It's an incredible product. And that's exactly the problem.

Every time I wanted to write a note, I had to decide: is this a task or a note? Does it go in the projects database or the journal? What properties does it need? Should I add a due date?

That moment of friction — the second it takes to decide where something goes before you write it — is enough for the thought to vanish. The idea flies before it lands.

The problem wasn't the power of the apps. It was that they asked me to think before capturing.

The brain doesn't work that way. Ideas don't arrive with labels or assigned properties. They come in the middle of a meeting, while driving, in the shower. And any system that asks you to organize them before saving them will always lose to the unfiltered moment.

The price that doesn't pay off

There's a very specific moment I had with Roam Research.

It was a Sunday afternoon. I looked at my credit card and saw the $15 charge. I opened Roam and realized I hadn't used it in three weeks.

$15 for nothing. What bothered me wasn't the money — it was the cycle. Pay, use for two weeks, abandon, feel guilty, cancel, find another app, repeat. Notion: $12. Obsidian Sync: $10. Craft: $5. If you switch frequently, it adds up. And the worst part is you pay for something you don't use because it takes too much effort to maintain.

The system that needed maintaining

The most insidious problem isn't that these apps are complicated to learn. It's that they're complicated to maintain.

Notion needs you to update databases when a project changes. To archive what's no longer relevant. To keep templates up to date. Obsidian needs you to manage plugins when they update. To check for broken links. To decide what to do with orphaned notes.

At some point, the system becomes the problem. Not only does it take your time to organize you — it asks for additional time to maintain the tool that was supposed to be saving you time.

Your notes are hostages

One day I decided to leave Notion. I'd had enough.

I exported everything. I ended up with a folder full of Markdown files with Notion frontmatter, images with broken URLs pointing to their servers, and a folder structure that made no sense outside Notion. Two hours trying to clean that up. I ended up importing a fraction of what I had.

That's lock-in: your notes are yours in theory, but in practice they're tied to the platform. The format is theirs, the images are on their servers, the internal links don't work anywhere else.

The AI that didn't help

When AI-powered apps arrived, I thought I'd finally found a solution.

Notion AI. Obsidian with GPT plugins. New apps promising «your second brain with AI». I tried them all.

The pattern was always the same: select text, press a button, the AI suggests something. Summarize, expand, rephrase. It works. But it doesn't solve the real problem.

The real problem isn't that I need AI to write for me. The problem is that I have hundreds of notes and can't find what I'm looking for. That I captured something three weeks ago and don't know where it is. That my tasks are scattered across ten different places.

I didn't want ChatGPT on top of my notes. I wanted something that knew my notes. That could tell me what I've written about project X without me having to search for it. That would classify on its own. That remembered who I am.

My notes in seven different places

At one point I had: Apple Notes for quick things on my phone, Notion for work projects, Bear for personal notes, a Google doc for shared stuff, voice memos I never went back to listen to, WhatsApp to myself for ideas on the go, and physical notebooks for meetings.

Seven different places. None complete. Everything fragmented.

If you don't know where something is, it's as if you never had it. And I didn't know where almost anything was.

The moment it became clear

The insight came in a very simple way: the problem wasn't me. It was that every app asked me to make decisions before letting me write.

What type is this? Where does it go? What properties does it have? Which folder?

The brain doesn't capture like that. Ideas arrive without structure. And if the first step of the system is to decide the structure, the system will always lose against unfiltered thought.

I wanted a tool that worked the opposite way: write first, organize later. That understands what you write without you having to explain it. That classifies on its own. That remembers. That acts.

How From was born

The first version of From was terrible. A simple outliner with no AI, no contexts, no sync. Just a tree of notes on the Mac.

I used it for three months to see if it held up. It did. The outliner — the idea that everything lives in a hierarchical tree you can expand and collapse — turned out to be the right format for how I think.

I kept adding things: the automatic journal (a note for each day, without creating it), sync between devices, search, and finally AI — built from within, not glued on top.

The most important thing I learned in the process: AI has to understand the user's context, not just the current text. That's why From builds a profile from what you write, learns the projects and people that appear frequently, and automatically classifies each note into the right context.

No menus. No decisions. You just write.

What From solves

Friction: in From you don't decide where something goes before writing it. It goes to today's journal, and From automatically classifies it into the right context. If you mention a project, it links it. If you write a date, it detects it.

Price: free plan with one thousand real nodes — no time limit, no arbitrarily locked features. If you decide to pay, it's €7/month. No commitment.

Maintenance: no databases to update, no templates to manage. The tree builds itself as you use the app.

Lock-in: automatic backup every two hours in standard Markdown on your Mac. Any editor can open it. If you decide to leave tomorrow, you take everything with you.

Useless AI: Magic has access to your entire vault. It doesn't rephrase text — it searches weeks of notes, classifies without being asked, remembers who you are.

Fragmentation: one tree for everything. Mac, iPhone and web synced in real time.

I've been using From daily for over a year. It's the first notes app that's lasted more than six months.

Not because it's perfect — there's a lot to improve. But because it doesn't ask anything of me before letting me write.
