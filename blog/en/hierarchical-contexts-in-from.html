Hierarchical contexts: organize your life without lifting a finger

Fromly understands that "La Isla" lives inside "Work". It classifies there — not in a flat tag.

Fromly Blog · 5 junio 2026 · 5 min read

The problem with flat organization

Most productivity tools give you tags. You create #work, #personal, #finance, and you tag everything you can. It works — until it doesn't. When your projects multiply, tags become noise. You end up with 40 tags, none of which truly capture where something lives.

The real world isn't flat. Your trading community lives inside your business. Your business lives inside your work. Your workout routine lives inside your health. Everything has a place, and that place has a parent. Flat tags ignore that entirely.

Fromly doesn't ask you to tag "La Isla" as #work. It already knows that "La Isla" lives inside "Work" — because you put it there. And it classifies everything accordingly.

How hierarchical contexts actually work in Fromly

In Fromly, every node you create lives somewhere in a tree. That tree isn't just a visual folder structure — it's the actual context model Fromly uses to understand your notes, tasks, and entries.

Your entire life in a tree — Fromly reads the hierarchy, not just the words.

When you write a task under "La Isla → Marketing → August campaign", From knows exactly what world that task belongs to. It doesn't need a tag. It doesn't need you to assign a category. The position in the tree is the classification.

This means:

  • Filtering works by context, not just by keyword. Ask Fromly to show you everything pending in "Work" and it pulls from every child node — all projects, all subprojects, all nested tasks — without you building a complex filter.
  • The daily note understands where things come from. When Fromly surfaces a reminder or resurfaces a note, it knows the parent context and presents it correctly.
  • Nothing gets orphaned. Tasks don't float in a flat list. They always belong somewhere meaningful.

A concrete example: your work life, structured

Say your tree looks like this:

  • Work
    • La Isla del Trading
      • Content
      • Record market report Thursday
      • Marketing
      • Review August email sequence
    • Media Sector
      • Client proposals
      • Mira mi Web — new site draft

Fromly sees all of this as a single connected map. When you're working on "La Isla" and you ask Fromly what's pending, it doesn't search your entire database blindly — it navigates the tree from that node down and returns only what's relevant. No configuration required.

Jump directly to any context from the sidebar — no searching, no scrolling.

Why this beats tags every time

Tags require you to do the classification work every time you create something. You have to remember your taxonomy. You have to apply it consistently. You have to update it when your projects change.

Hierarchical contexts in Fromly flip this. You structure your tree once — loosely, naturally, the way you think about your life — and Fromly inherits that structure for everything you put inside it. New task under "La Isla → Content"? It's already classified. You didn't do anything extra.

The best organization system is the one that works without you thinking about it. In Fromly, the tree is the system — and it's already running.

What this looks like in practice

  1. Build your top-level nodes once. Work, Personal, Health, Finance — or whatever matches how you actually think. Don't overthink it.
  2. Put things where they naturally belong. If it's about La Isla, it goes under La Isla. Fromly handles the rest.
  3. Use the sidebar shortcuts to jump between contexts instantly. No searching, no filtering setup.
  4. Let filters do the heavy lifting. Fromly's filters read the hierarchy — filter by parent node and everything underneath surfaces automatically.

The planner knows your context too

When you open the planner in Fromly, tasks don't appear stripped of their origin. Fromly shows you where each task lives — which project, which context, which branch of your tree. This is critical when you're reviewing your day: you're not looking at a decontextualized list, you're looking at your actual work, organized the way you organized your life.

Every task carries its context — you always know where it lives and why it matters.

This is what separates Fromly from tools that give you a daily view with no memory of where things came from. Context isn't an afterthought. It's the foundation.

Conclusion

Hierarchical contexts in Fromly aren't a feature you configure — they're how Fromly thinks. Put things where they belong, and Fromly understands your entire structure without friction. Stop managing tags. Start building a tree that works.
